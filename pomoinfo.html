Background Information

The Pomodoro Technique

This working timer is based on the Pomodoro time management technique, developed by Francesco Cirillo.

The basic idea is to break down your task into working intervals of 25 minutes (one pomodoro). Between the pomodori, have a short 5 minutes pause. After four pomodori, take a longer break of about 15 - 20 minutes.

Tasks can be organized into pomodoro projects. Such a project shouldn't take up more than 5 - 7 pomodori. If it would take more time, chances are the task is too complex and should be broken down into seperate activities of a larger project.

Within each pomodoro, try to stay focused and to ignore all kinds of distraction. If you feel the urge to look into your emails, to water your plants, or to google something --- jot it down to make sure you do not forget about it, and wait for the break to work through your list: Either the activities on the list do not require more than 5 minutes - then do it immediately -, or assign a pomodoro project to it. But it's important that you use the break to get away from your current task and do something different. Stand up, walk around, or jump into the pool!

Working with a timer can thus help you stay focused.

Navigation

After you started a timer, you can stop the timer and return to the starting screen by pressing the escape-key (or touch the screen).

You can't, however, pause the timer. That would defeat the object of the pomodoro! (The basic rule in the Pomodoro Technique is: "Once a pomodoro started, the timer has to ring!") If you really have to stop the pomodoro for an interruption --- then end the timer, take the 5 minutes break, reschedule your pomodoro projects, and restart the timer after the break.
